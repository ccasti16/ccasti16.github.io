Project 1: Relationship between Race, Gender, and Political Party of Supreme Court Justices

Problem Definition

Are there trends with the appointment of Supreme Court Justices in relation to the relationship between race, gender, and political party?

There are many different characteristics of Supreme Court Justices that can affect their appointment and its success. There has been a prior correlation shown between the race and gender of Justices and the likelihood of being nominated and subsequently appointed. Nominations to the Court are given by the President, so this research seeks to see if there are correlations between the likelihood of Justices with a certain gender or race getting nominated, depending on their political party. In recent years, there has been more diversity among the Court; a correlation between those individuals and the likelihood of which party they would belong to can provide insight into how the Court can continue to grow.

Data Description

This data comes from Stonegroove’s “Supreme Court Demographic Database” on Kaggle (2021). This dataset is a subset of a much larger dataset from Epstein et al.’s “The U.S. Supreme Court Justices Database(2021).” Each row represents a different Justice in order of their appointment to the Court. Some of the columns in this subset are the Justices’ names, their date of birth, the state they were born in, their gender, their race, their religion, the state in which they lived, their political party, and the president who nominated the Justice. The key columns for the question at hand are the name column, the race column, the gender column, and the political party column. Considering this, assumptions will be made about the time periods of the date of birth of the Justices. Thurgood Marshall, who is data point 98 out of 117, will be considered the beginning of the period in which there was social progress that people of color and women could be considered for the Court, as he was appointed three years after the passing of the Civil Rights Act. It should be noted that with the shifts in the types of dominant political parties over the years, there will be subgroups with additional information needed based on the time period. From John Mclean, data point 22, until Earl Warren, data point 90, these data points will serve to represent the period of time in which the Democrats and Republicans had opposite values of the current understanding of the parties today. From John Jay, data point 1, to Robert Trimble, data point 21, the political parties are considered too different from the values of modern-day Republicans or Democrats to be remodeled as one, so their data will be observed separately, to reflect the correlation within the principles of the power and type of government within the founding-era period.

Data Cleaning & Preparation

As mentioned above, the values within the political parties can have different meanings based on the time period(U.S. Embassy in Denmark, n.d.), so for this research, they will be reclassified for easier understanding of the values a modern audience will understand at first glance. All data points between 22 and 90 will be switched, disregarding data point 33(Whig). Any Justice between that period who was a Democrat will be classified as a Republican, and any vice-versa. This is to reflect the party switch between the 1940s and 1960s that was mentioned above. This will make the data more readable and cohesive for an understanding of the results. This will trade off the factual title of their party they had during their period on the Court, but will better reflect their values. To give historical understanding and recognition to the lack of freedoms given to people of color and women throughout history, the data will be rerun separately with a subgroup of data points 98 to 117. This is to represent the time period during and after people of color and women began to formally be recognized by the government to receive the same freedoms as white men. This subgroup will not be perfect to represent the smaller subgroups of when white women and women of color began to receive those rights and freedoms later on. To have more data points to work with, these different civil rights movements will be grouped together for the understanding of a period in which multiple minority groups began to have more formal opportunities.

Data Understanding & Visualization

Heatmaps and bar graphs were initially used to visualize the data, but as the research progressed, both of the visualizations told the same story, so only the bar graphs were removed for the sake of the size of the project. The heatmap showed a better correlation between the different variables because in the initial subgroup, the number of people of color and women was so low that it was barely noticeable. The heatmap showed the same correlation, but allowed for better visualization. Not many patterns or trends were surprising due to the limited data based on the history and the unlimited term of Supreme Court Justices. There were higher rates of white males in both categories, even after creating a new subgroup to account for after the Civil Rights Act had been passed, but that was to be expected.

Storytelling & Interpretation

The findings show that there was no significant correlation between either race or gender in correlation to political party in either subgroup. When looking at the heatmaps, it does seem as if there is a slight majority of women in the post-Civil Rights Act subgroup that are registered as Democrats, but due to the lack of further data, there can be no significant result. This doesn’t mean that there isn’t some possible correlation, just that it is too soon after the Civil Rights Act to see any significant impact or correlation with parties. The heatmaps show that there may be a trend beginning with more people of color and women in the democratic party, but with how few members there are, and the fact that multiple Justices fit into multiple minority categories of race and gender, there needs to be more data to find a result. As time moves forward, more work can be done to have a more diverse Court, while still balancing all of the other requirements of being appointed. It would be misleading to conclude anything from this data since all of the p-values from each test were above 0.5.

Limitations, Ethics & Reflection

Considering the unlimited terms and relatively short history of the United States, there is limited data available in this dataset to make a statistical conclusion. Despite creating subgroups to account for the long history in which people of color and women did not have the same freedoms and opportunities to run for the court, there is still far too little Justices and diversity to conclude anything. The heatmaps show interesting data and slight curves to democratic Justices being more even in diversity after the Civil Rights Act was passed; however, all of the significance testing still shows no significant correlation between either gender or race in relation to political party.

The major assumption made in this research was that switching the “Republican” and “Democrat” values over the course of about sixty Justices. The decision to swap the classifications came from a need to clarify the data more, as the values and definitions of the parties swapped over time. However, if there was any outlier within that time period who was listed as their political party, yet had the values that we currently attribute to it in this modern day, their data could be skewed.

The data was not very surprising. The research was conducted with the knowledge that there would not be enough data to conclude anything statistically significant. However, it was a research question that was authentic to be considered with the current trend of the Court.

Ideally, this research could be redone in a hundred years when there are far more Justices since the Civil Rights Act. If there were more time to do this research now, the most interesting variable to add would be the time each justice served on the Court. Certain justices have served for very long times on the Court; it would be an intriguing study to see if there is a correlation between party, which is more likely to sit on the Court for longer.

Project 2: Predicting Supreme Court Justice Votes Using Machine Learning

Problem Definition

Can a machine learning model predict how a Supreme Court justice will vote on a case based on who they are and what type of case is before them?

Individual justices carry distinct judicial philosophies, ideological leanings, and records across different areas of law. Judges can view cases through a more orginalistic or with the opposite view of a living constitutional point of view, for example. At the same time, certain case types consistently produce more divided Courts than others. This project asks whether those patterns are strong enough that a classifier can learn them: given a justice's identity and the category of a case, can the model predict whether they will vote with the majority, dissent, or take some other position?

Understanding judicial voting patterns has real implications for legal strategy, policy advocacy, and democratic accountability. It also raises important questions about what it means to predict human judgment with an algorithm.

Data Description

This project uses the Supreme Court Database (SCDB), version 2025_01, justice-centered and organized by legal provision. The SCDB is maintained by Harold J. Spaeth and colleagues and is one of the most comprehensive datasets on U.S. Supreme Court decisions, spanning cases from 1946 to the present term.

The full dataset contains 124,797 rows, where each row represents a single justice's vote on a single case. There are 61 columns covering case identifiers, citation information, dates, case characteristics, issue classifications, and justice-level voting data. The target variable for this project is vote, which takes values 1 through 8. 1 indicates a vote with the majority, 2 indicates a dissent, and values 3–8 represent concurrences, judgments, and other specialized vote types.

The key predictive features retained for modeling were justice (numeric ID), justiceName (string, encoded to integer), and issueArea (one of 14 broad legal categories such as Criminal Procedure, Civil Rights, and First Amendment), supplemented by case-level context variables including decisionDirection, lcDisposition, certReason, jurisdiction, partyWinning, majVotes, minVotes, and decisionType.

Data Cleaning & Preparation

Of the 61 original columns, only 13 were retained for modeling. Columns removed included all case identifiers (IDs, docket numbers, citation strings), free-text fields, date columns, and post-decision variables that would leak outcome information into the features. The remaining columns were selected because they are likely available at or before decision time.

3,451 rows were missing the vote target variable and were dropped, leaving 121,346 usable rows. The string column justiceName was encoded to an integer using pd.factorize(). The remaining missing values in feature columns were filled using column medians rather than dropping rows, with the goal of preserving as much data as possible.

The vote distribution is heavily imbalanced: vote=1 accounts for 85,734 rows (~71%), vote=2 for 22,513 (~19%), and all other vote types combined for the remaining ~10%. This imbalance is the central challenge for all three models.

Modeling Approach

Three classifiers were trained and compared, each representing a different algorithmic family:

Decision Tree

A single decision tree with max_depth=10 and min_samples_leaf=20 to prevent overfitting. Decision trees are fully interpretable — the top three levels can be visualized to understand what splits the model makes first. This model is meant to serve as a baseline of comparison for future models.

Random Forest

An ensemble of 100 decision trees (n_estimators=100), using the same depth and leaf constraints. By aggregating predictions across many trees trained on random subsets of the data, random forests reduce the variance that makes a single tree unstable. This model is expected to be the most accurate but least interpretable.

K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN)

A distance-based classifier with k=9 neighbors. Because KNN is sensitive to feature scale, the input features were standardized with StandardScaler before fitting. In cross-validation, the scaler was wrapped inside a Pipeline so that it is fit only on training folds, preventing data leakage. KNN makes predictions based on the similarity of a case-justice pair to historical examples, making it a fundamentally different approach from the tree-based models.

All models were evaluated on an 80/20 train-test split (stratified by random seed 42) and validated further with 5-fold cross-validation.

Results

Accuracy & Weighted Metrics

All three models achieved similar test-set accuracy in the range of 75–76%. However, the gap between weighted F1 (~0.70) and macro F1 (~0.29–0.43) reveals that this accuracy is largely driven by correct predictions on the dominant vote=1 class. A model that predicted "majority" for every case would achieve roughly 71% accuracy — so the models are adding only about 4–5 percentage points above that baseline.

Per-Class Performance

Vote=1 was predicted well by all three models (recall of 92–98%). Vote=2 (dissent) was the hardest meaningful class: the best recall achieved was 43% (Decision Tree and KNN), while Random Forest only caught 30% of dissents. For rarer vote types (3–7), Random Forest scored 0% recall on most classes, effectively never predicting them. KNN performed the best on minority classes — achieving 12% recall on vote=3, 15% on vote=4 — and had the highest macro F1 (0.4275), meaning it was the most balanced across all vote types. Vote=8 was predicted with near-perfect accuracy by all three models, suggesting it has a very distinctive feature signature.

Cross-Validation

5-fold CV confirmed Random Forest as the most stable model (accuracy 72.44% ± 1.62%), followed by Decision Tree (71.78% ± 2.69%) and KNN (67.84% ± 2.84%). KNN's larger drop from single-split to CV accuracy suggests it benefited somewhat from the particular 80/20 split used in the main evaluation.

The class imbalance in the SCDB — where majority votes outnumber all other types roughly 7:1 — is the binding constraint on all three models. Addressing it through techniques such as class_weight='balanced' or SMOTE oversampling would be the most impactful next step for improving predictions on dissents and concurrences.

Ethics & Reflection

Predicting judicial votes raises meaningful ethical concerns beyond model performance. Judges are people, not data points, and reducing a justice's decision to a predicted output based on their identity and a case category risks flattening the complexity of legal reasoning into a statistical pattern. The model does not know what arguments were made, how briefs were written, or what values a justice weighed. The model only knows how a Justice voted in a certain way for certain cases in the past.

There is also a risk of self-fulfilling inference. If litigants use predictive tools to select which arguments to make or which courts to pursue, model predictions could subtly shape the very outcomes they claim to merely forecast. However, it should be noted that this is a common practice taught throughout all levels of law, even in Undergraduate Mock Trial, where litigants are taught to “read the judge” to determine the outcome of a case before it has even started. This feedback loop is especially concerning when the model disproportionately fails minority vote classes, meaning it is most uncertain in the cases that differ from the usual cases the Court sees. It is those cases that tend to be the most controversial and make major news outlets.

The heavy class imbalance in this dataset is itself a reflection of how the Court works. In the majority of cases, the Court sees over produce large majorities since there is little disagreement in the fundamentals of the law, which are, in most cases. A model trained primarily on those cases will learn to reproduce consensus, not disagreement. What scholars and academics would want to predict are those dissents.

However, the act of predicting a Justice’s vote is done for every major case the Court is set to see. Academics weigh in with their expertise on every little aspect of the case and their understanding of the Justices and their specific ideologies in the courtroom. Academics fill in the gap of knowledge and understanding that the models fail to see, while the models can examine trends for a vast amount of cases that an academic cannot visualize the connections between. This model can be improved, but only in its best use with an academic within the field of law who can fill in the gaps in the understanding of the nuances of the courtroom and briefs.

Project 3: Birds of a Feather — Who Picks Your Team? Building a Fairer Way to Form Student Project Groups

The moment everyone dreads

There is a small, predictable silence in every college classroom when an instructor says the words "For this next project, you'll be in groups of four." Some students immediately turn to the friends they came in with. Others freeze, hoping the instructor will assign teams so they don't have to navigate the social cost of asking strangers. A few quietly recalculate the rest of their semester — who is going to do the work? who has my schedule? who's going to ghost the group chat?

Group projects are everywhere in higher education, and yet the way teams get formed has barely changed in decades. Instructors typically choose between two options: let students pick (which reproduces existing social cliques and excludes newcomers), or assign randomly (which ignores the very things that determine whether a team functions — schedule, skill mix, work style). Neither option is satisfying, and neither scales well to a class of 30 students juggling jobs, commutes, and conflicting deadlines.

So we asked the obvious question: could a data-driven approach do better?

That question turned into the Teammate Matcher — a recommendation tool, not an autonomous decision-maker, that uses machine learning to surface team configurations an instructor can review and adjust.

Why this matters beyond a class assignment

It is tempting to file "team formation" under minor logistical headache, but the choice of who works with whom is pedagogically consequential. Group composition shapes who learns from whom, who gets credit, who carries silent labor, and — at the limit — who passes a course (Kyprianidou et al., 2012). When those decisions are made by algorithm rather than by hand, the same set of questions that animates broader debates about AI in high-stakes domains shows up in miniature: Whose values are encoded in the model? Whose data does it use, and with what consent? Can the people affected by it understand what it's doing? (O'Neil, 2016; Akgun & Greenhow, 2022)

Higher education has spent the last decade quietly importing data-driven decision systems — early-warning dashboards, automated tutoring placements, "engagement" analytics drawn from learning-management click streams. Most of these systems run without the consent of the students they classify and without the affected instructors fully understanding how they work. We wanted to take the opposite approach: build something narrow, transparent, opt-in, and explicitly designed to keep the instructor in charge.

Three questions, one project

Our work was guided by three research questions:

  1. How can we represent students' skills, availability, and work styles using direct self-report — and is that data rich enough to drive useful matches?
  2. Which clustering or optimization algorithms produce the most balanced teams — and does the answer change when we optimize for similar teams versus complementary ones?
  3. Which student attributes actually drive cluster separation? In other words, what is the algorithm really listening to?

The third question is the most important one for understanding what the algorithm means. A model that produces good-looking teams but listens primarily to GPA, or to one cultural style of self-presentation, has different ethical implications than one that listens primarily to schedule overlap. Knowing which features dominate is not optional — it is the audit.

How we built it

We deployed an anonymous Google Forms survey to 31 students in a single section of DTSC 2302. The survey collected 35 items across five domains: schedule and availability, technical skills (8 dimensions, self-rated 1–5), work style, communication preferences, and a self-assessment block. After preprocessing — checkbox expansion, ordinal encoding, one-hot encoding, Min-Max normalization, and a privacy-preserving row shuffle — we ended up with 50 features per student and zero missing values.

We then ran four different models on the cleaned data, deliberately chosen to span the design space:

  • K-Means clustering — the standard workhorse, fast and interpretable, but it cannot enforce balanced team sizes.
  • Agglomerative (Ward) clustering — a hierarchical alternative that produces a dendrogram for instructor inspection and makes no spherical-cluster assumption.
  • Hungarian Algorithm assignment — a combinatorial optimization technique from operations research (Kuhn, 1955), used to enforce that every team contains exactly 3–6 students, a property no standard clustering algorithm guarantees.
  • Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) — a probabilistic model that produces soft assignments instead of hard cluster labels (Bishop, 2006), letting us flag ambiguous students for human review.

The first three models were applied to a similarity feature set (availability + work style). GMM was applied to a complementarity feature set (skills only). We evaluated all four models on six metrics: three algorithmic (Silhouette Score, Davies–Bouldin, Calinski–Harabasz) and three domain-specific (intra-team skill variance, schedule overlap via Jaccard similarity, and skill coverage).

What we found

1. The Hungarian Algorithm is the model we'd actually deploy

It is the only one of the four that guaranteed every team contained between three and six students — the other models routinely produced oversized teams (one K-Means cluster contained 10 students) or two-person teams too small to cover the project's required skills. It also tied for the highest skill coverage (7.875 out of 8 skill dimensions per team). Its silhouette score is the lowest of the four, which is the explicit cost of forcing balanced sizes — but for a deployment context, balanced and slightly-less-tight is far more useful than tight-but-unusable.

2. Schedule, not skill, is what separates students

When we ran Principal Component Analysis on the full feature set, the first principal component (15.8% of variance) was dominated by day-of-week availability — weekend, Tuesday, and Thursday loadings positive; Monday loading negative. The second component (13.5%) was conflict-handling and meeting-mode preferences. Self-rated skill features did not appear in the top loadings until the third principal component.

Within this cohort, students differentiate primarily on when they can meet and how they handle conflict — not on what they say they're good at. An algorithm that spends its energy matching on skills will have very little signal to work with.

3. GPA is not a neutral feature

We ran K-Means with and without gpa_band and compared resulting cluster labels using the Adjusted Rand Index. The score was 0.34 — moderately low. Removing GPA actually improved the schedule-overlap metric slightly (0.628 versus 0.604). GPA was actively pulling the clustering away from its primary objective.

4. GMM produced sharp, not soft, archetypes

Every one of the 31 students locked tightly onto exactly one of the eight latent skill archetypes with probability ≈ 1.0. Zero students were flagged as ambiguous. This is not a failure of the algorithm — it is a property of the data. In a larger sample the ambiguity-flag mechanism would be exercised meaningfully; in this run, it had nothing to surface.

The uneven availability across the cohort — weekday afternoons dense, weekends and late-night sparse — is the binding constraint on all three similarity-based models. A larger or more temporally diverse sample would be the highest-value next step.

The ethics, named explicitly

Self-report bias is a fairness problem

Students from cultures or backgrounds where confidence-expression is discouraged will systematically under-rate themselves on a 1-to-5 Likert scale (Akgun & Greenhow, 2022). An algorithm that takes those self-ratings at face value will route those students into "low-skill" team slots. The Hungarian Algorithm's size-balancing constraint mitigates this by forcing mixed teams, and our skill-coverage metric specifically asks whether anyone on the team meets each threshold. But the underlying bias remains in the data — we can refuse to amplify it; we can't fix it.

Algorithmic recommendations create anchoring pressure

Even with explicit human-in-the-loop framing, an instructor presented with a confident-looking algorithmic output is more likely to accept it than to override it (Akgun & Greenhow, 2022). To counter this, we deliberately produce multiple configurations rather than one, surface ambiguity flags from GMM, and present per-metric trade-offs rather than a single composite score. The point is to make scrutiny structurally easier than rubber-stamping.

Anonymous data isn't always anonymous

Our raw survey data was collected anonymously — no names, no emails, no student IDs. But the Google Forms export included an exact-second timestamp for each submission, and in a class of 31 students who know each other, "who submitted at 11:30 AM on Wednesday" can identify someone. We treat the timestamp as a quasi-identifier: the raw file stays local and is never published, and the processed file is row-shuffled with a fixed random seed before saving, breaking the correlation between row position and submission order. Reproducibility is preserved; re-identification is not.

Human in the loop, on purpose

The Teammate Matcher does not assign teams. It produces a set of candidate configurations with interpretable metrics — schedule overlap, skill coverage, ambiguity flags — and the instructor makes the final call. We made this choice deliberately, not as a hedge.

There are things the algorithm cannot see: known interpersonal conflicts, prior team history, accommodation needs, students currently navigating difficult life circumstances, the instructor's pedagogical intent for that particular project. An autonomous system that overrides these is not a better system — it is a brittle one. The right place for an algorithm in this problem is upstream of the decision, surfacing options and quantifying trade-offs, not making the call.

What we'd do next

The most important piece of future work is validation: we need to know whether algorithmically formed teams actually report less friction and produce better outcomes than randomly assigned ones. That requires a post-project survey on team satisfaction, ideally run across multiple sections. Until that data exists, we have shown only that the algorithm produces plausibly good teams by our chosen metrics — not that it produces measurably better teams in lived experience.

We also plan to implement the equity constraint we currently document but don't enforce: no team should be composed entirely of students who self-rate below threshold on any single skill dimension. And we want to compare Euclidean distance with Gower's similarity, which handles mixed binary/continuous data more cleanly than either does individually.

A small claim

We started this project asking whether a data-driven tool could replace instructor judgment in team formation. We finished it convinced that the right framing is the inverse: a data-driven tool can protect instructor judgment by surfacing the trade-offs that hand-assignment makes implicit. The model is a magnifying glass, not a verdict. That distinction is small, but it is the entire ethical difference between a recommendation system and an automation system, and it is the difference we tried to design for.

Introductory Reflection: Why study data science in social and interdisciplinary contexts?

Published Jan, 2026 — by Carly C.

Learning data science in social and interdisciplinary contexts matters to me because the questions we choose to model and the ways we interpret results shape real-world outcomes. Modeling social topics and people is not the same as modeling machines or physical sensors. Models about people can have many different meanings and aspects of life that lead to the data. Understanding those meanings can help avoid misinterpretation of data and people.

One reason to model social topics is to create better-informed decisions. Thoughtful analysis can reveal patterns or disparities that support more equitable policies, resource allocation, or service design. But modeling also brings risks: simplified models can reify stereotypes. For that reason, technical skill must be paired with ethical reflection and engagement with domain experts and affected communities.

At this point, some responsibilities that come to mind include protecting privacy, avoiding overclaiming what analyses show, and acknowledging uncertainty, especially within social groups. There is a duty to document data origins, limitations, choices made in cleaning and modeling, and mistakes along the road. Researchers and practitioners should consider who are the stakeholders at the end of day and who might be harmed by possible generalization of data. Openness about methods and sensitivity to consent and context are practical and required steps toward responsible work.

Through this course I hope to better understand practical ways to combine quantitative methods with qualitative insight. I hope to look at data in a more human way: a way models can't. I want to get better at coding and feeling confident in my decisions with data analysis.

An Analysis of the Social Determinants of Health and the Capabilities Approach

Published Feb, 2026 — by Carly C.

The Social Determinants of Health are economic stability, healthcare access and quality, education access and quality, social and community context, and neighborhood and built environment.

Economic stability is a determinant of health as the foundation to being able to steadily afford things like housing and healthcare. If someone is working a minimum wage job and cannot afford housing in their city, and does not receive healthcare through their work or any other agency, it can lower their other determinants of health. Healthcare access and the quality of that access can also determine the quality of someone’s health by affordable access to medical care and medications. If someone does not have healthcare and gets a virus that needs antibiotics, but cannot afford a trip to the doctor, along with the cost of the medication, they will likely suffer for longer and be out of work for longer. Education access and the quality of that access is one of the more social parts of the determinants of health, as higher levels of education quality and years tend to increase the other factors of health. For example, a student who had affordable access to college went on to get a well-paying job, which led them to afford healthcare and a neighborhood with less crime. Their health will be better than someone who did not have access to that job because they did not have that same education. Social and community contexts affect an individual’s health through the quality of the social life with their family and friends. Someone who doesn’t engage much with their community and loved ones, could have a higher chance at developing depression and anxiety, thereby affecting their other quality of determinants of health. Neighborhood and built environment are health determinants since there are things like the quality of the air, the quality of the water, and crime rates that can affect an individual. In Flint, Michigan, there was an ongoing battle with the state to fix the quality of the water that had harmful effects on the individuals in that town, and added an extra burden of cost and living to avoid.

The Capabilities Approach is a way of determining well-being by more than just access to resources and income, but by what individuals are capable of doing. These capabilities are often freedoms to do and act as someone pleases in regards to their life, bodily health, and education. Someone should have the freedom to choose what food they would eat, whether it is healthier or not.
